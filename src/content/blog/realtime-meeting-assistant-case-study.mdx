## Problem Statement

Enterprise teams run high-stakes meetings where decisions, blockers, and ownership assignments are frequently buried in long conversation streams. Raw transcripts are useful, but they are not enough. The actual value comes from extracting clear outcomes that teams can act on immediately.

The system we are designing is a **real-time meeting assistant** that must:
- stream live captions,
- maintain speaker-attributed transcript state,
- generate post-meeting summary artifacts,
- extract actionable tasks with owner and confidence,
- do all of this under strict tenant isolation and compliance boundaries.

### Who uses this system

Primary users are meeting participants who need immediate clarity and reliable post-meeting follow-up.

Secondary users are operations, compliance, and platform teams who need auditability, policy controls, and predictable production behavior.

### Why this is a hard systems problem

You are balancing three constraints that naturally fight each other:
- **latency**: live UX breaks quickly when updates lag,
- **quality**: summaries and action items must be grounded and useful,
- **trust**: enterprise tenants need strict data boundaries and explainability.

Most failures in production happen at the boundaries between these constraints, not inside a single model call.

## Framing the System

Before architecture, define hard interfaces.

### Core Inputs
- live meeting audio stream
- meeting identifiers (`tenant_id`, `meeting_id`)
- participant roster metadata
- rolling transcript context and speaker timeline

### Core Outputs
- live captions
- speaker-attributed transcript segments with timestamps
- final meeting summary
- structured action items (`owner`, `task`, optional `due_date`, `confidence`)

### Non-Negotiable Constraints
- low-latency live updates
- strict tenant data isolation
- PII-safe handling and policy-based retention
- output lineage that can be traced back to transcript evidence

### What this system is not

It is not a generic chatbot attached to meeting text. It is a **streaming intelligence pipeline** where speech models, language models, state stores, and deterministic controls must cooperate under load.

## Step 0: Why GenAI?

This is the first design decision that should be explicit.

A deterministic speech stack can produce captions and transcripts. It cannot reliably produce high-quality semantic summaries and action extraction in the face of overlapping speech, implicit decisions, domain jargon, and context shifts.

### Where deterministic systems are enough
- stream ingestion and ordering
- auth and tenant policy enforcement
- schema validation
- retention and deletion controls

### Where GenAI is needed
- semantic compression of long conversational context
- extracting action items from implicit language
- preserving intent across multi-topic discussions
- converting noisy conversation into clean decision summaries

### Cost and value framing

If this system is used only for transcript display, GenAI cost is difficult to justify. If the product promise includes reliable action extraction and high-quality summaries at scale, GenAI provides direct value.

The decision is not "LLM or no LLM." The decision is **which parts of the pipeline should be deterministic and which parts should be generative**.

### Practical architecture principle from Step 0

Use GenAI where ambiguity exists. Use deterministic controls where invariants must hold.

This principle will drive every later decision:
- model routing,
- guardrails,
- schema enforcement,
- evaluation and monitoring.

## Step 1: Requirements

If requirements are vague here, every later decision gets hand-wavy. This is the section where we lock explicit targets.

### Functional requirements

- Stream live captions continuously during the meeting.
- Maintain speaker-attributed transcript with timestamps.
- Produce a final post-meeting summary.
- Extract structured action items with `owner`, `task`, optional `due_date`, and `confidence`.

### Non-functional requirements

- **Latency**
  - live caption TTFT under 1 second at P95
  - caption refresh cadence every 1 to 3 seconds
  - final summary available under 30 seconds after meeting end at P95
- **Availability**
  - 99.9% for caption and transcript path
- **Compliance**
  - strict tenant-scoped data access
  - PII-safe logging and configurable retention policy
- **Reliability**
  - no silent extraction failures
  - deterministic fallback behavior under model or infra pressure

### Scale assumptions

- Peak concurrency: 10k to 50k meetings
- Average meeting duration: 30 to 60 minutes
- Average participant count: 4 to 12
- Load shape: calendar-driven bursts (top of hour)

These assumptions are not exact. They are enough to drive capacity and architecture choices.

### Technical evaluation metrics

- End-to-end caption latency (`audio chunk -> caption displayed`) P50/P95
- Stage latency breakdown: ingest, ASR, diarization, assembly, summary
- Queue backlog age and drop rate by stage
- Cost per processed audio minute
- Structured output schema-validity rate for action items

### Model quality metrics

- `WER` (Word Error Rate): transcription word error ratio, lower is better
- `DER` (Diarization Error Rate): speaker assignment error ratio, lower is better
- `Faithfulness`: fraction of summary claims supported by transcript evidence, higher is better
- `Omission rate`: fraction of important decisions/action items missing from summary, lower is better
- Action-item extraction precision, recall, and F1

### Hard trade-offs to acknowledge early

| Decision axis | Option A | Option B | Cost of getting it wrong |
|---|---|---|---|
| Live latency vs summary quality | faster model tier | higher quality tier | either laggy UX or untrusted output |
| One model for all stages vs stage-specific stack | simpler deployment | better latency-cost-quality control | overspend or chronic quality variance |
| Aggressive batching vs responsiveness | better throughput | better live UX | hidden caption lag under load |
| Strict guardrails vs extraction recall | fewer unsafe outputs | more aggressive extraction | either compliance risk or low utility |

A frequent interview miss is pretending these trade-offs do not exist. They always exist. The design quality is in how explicitly you define and govern them.

## Step 2: Architecture

For this system, the first architecture decision is boundary design: which services own stream orchestration, which layer owns model execution, and where failure isolation is enforced.

The first decision is to separate application orchestration from model execution. If each service owns its own runtime logic, the system drifts fast: retry behavior differs by service, model rollout logic becomes inconsistent, and cost controls are scattered. A centralized serving layer avoids that. Application services stay focused on workflow and state. Model routing, fallback, and concurrency control stay in one place.

The architecture shape is straightforward. On the application side, there are four services: `Ingest Gateway Service`, `Transcript Pipeline Service`, `Meeting Intelligence Service`, and `Guardrails Service`. On the inference side, there is one `Model Serving Gateway Service` fronting separate pools for `ASR`, `Diarization`, `Fast LLM`, and `Quality LLM`. Data is split across `Redis` for hot meeting state, `S3` for durable artifacts, and `Postgres` for queryable metadata and lineage.

What matters is not the number of boxes, it is the boundary quality.

`Ingest Gateway Service` should stay simple and highly available. Its job is to terminate streams, validate tenant/session context, normalize chunks, and hand off quickly. Any heavy processing here creates a single chokepoint for live UX.

`Transcript Pipeline Service` is where speech outputs become product state. It orchestrates ASR and diarization through serving gateway, merges outputs into speaker-attributed transcript segments, and writes rolling state into Redis. If this service lags, caption latency is the first visible symptom.

`Meeting Intelligence Service` should consume stabilized transcript state, not raw noisy partial churn. It handles rolling summaries during the meeting and the final post-meeting pass. It writes artifacts to S3 and metadata pointers to Postgres. If it fails, the transcript path should still continue; this is a quality-path service, not a hard dependency for basic meeting continuity.

`Guardrails Service` is policy and correctness control, not a “nice to have.” It validates structured outputs, checks confidence/evidence conditions, and decides whether to publish, degrade, or hold for review. Guardrail failure should block unsafe outputs but should not crash transcript delivery.

The second architectural decision is to explicitly split the pipeline into realtime and finalization paths. Realtime path optimizes for low-latency continuity. Finalization path optimizes for completeness and precision. Treating these as one path with one prompt/model strategy is a common mistake, and it usually produces bad results in both directions.

| Path | Input profile | Output profile | Primary optimization |
|---|---|---|---|
| Realtime | continuous chunk stream | incremental captions and rolling updates | latency stability |
| Finalization | full transcript snapshot | complete summary and structured extraction | quality and completeness |

A practical failure policy should follow the same split. Under load, preserve transcript continuity first, then speaker attribution, then rolling summary freshness, and only then final artifact speed. Publishing confident but weak outputs is more damaging than delaying polished outputs.

From a scaling perspective, speech pools and text-generation pools should scale independently because their pressure signatures differ. ASR is chunk-driven and steady under active meetings. Quality LLM finalization is burstier and prefill-heavy near meeting end events. Keeping them coupled in one autoscaling envelope hides bottlenecks and causes avoidable overprovisioning.

This is the core point of Step 2: architecture is a control system around competing goals, not a diagram exercise. If boundaries are clear, later decisions in data strategy, model selection, and ops become mechanical instead of opinion-driven.
